COMPLIANCE 6-Rule Compliant

PURPOSE

This document provides guidance on how to scale audience definition rigor based on objective type, budget size, and risk profile. It helps the agent determine when to push for more sophistication and when simpler approaches are appropriate. All audience definitions must include census-validated sizing and platform taxonomy codes for activation.

CORE PRINCIPLE

Audience sophistication should match the precision required by the objective. Brand building with broad reach requires less targeting precision than performance marketing with tight efficiency requirements. Over-targeting can reduce scale. Under-targeting can waste budget. The goal is appropriate precision with activation-ready outputs.

SOPHISTICATION TIERS

Tier 1 represents broad reach appropriate for brand and upper-funnel objectives. Signal count is approximately 5 to 10. Focus is on basic demographic alignment, broad interest categories, contextual relevance, and geographic targeting. This tier is appropriate when the objective is awareness or consideration, the audience is broad by design, budget is sufficient for wide reach, and learning is more valuable than immediate efficiency.

Tier 2 represents moderate precision appropriate for mid-funnel and consideration objectives. Signal count is approximately 10 to 20. Focus is on more specific behavioral signals, purchase intent indicators, category interest, competitive consideration, and life-stage targeting. This tier is appropriate when the objective is consideration or preference, the audience has identifiable characteristics, efficiency matters but is not the primary constraint, and some testing budget exists.

Tier 3 represents high precision appropriate for performance and lower-funnel objectives. Signal count is approximately 20 to 40 where feasible. Focus is on strong intent signals, recency indicators, predictive modeling, custom audiences, lookalike expansion from high-value customers, and cross-device and cross-channel coordination. This tier is appropriate when the objective is conversion or revenue, efficiency targets are tight, data is available to support precision, and measurement can attribute results.

SIGNAL CATEGORIES

Demographic signals include age, gender, income, education, occupation, household composition, and life stage. These are foundational but often insufficient alone. Over-reliance on demographics when behavioral data exists is a common mistake. Census data provides the base population for demographic filtering.

Behavioral signals include past purchase behavior, browsing history, engagement patterns, content consumption, search behavior, and app usage. These are generally more predictive than demographics. Availability varies by platform and data partnerships. Google In-Market and Meta Behaviors are primary sources.

Contextual signals include content adjacency, time of day, device type, location context, and environmental factors. These matter for brand safety and relevance. Increasingly important in privacy-constrained environments. IAB Content Taxonomy provides standard categorization.

Intent signals include search queries, comparison behavior, cart abandonment, pricing page visits, and review reading. These are the strongest performance indicators. Limited availability and often expensive to acquire.

Psychographic signals include values, attitudes, interests, lifestyle, and brand affinity. Useful for message alignment and creative strategy. Often less reliable than behavioral signals. Meta Interests and Google Affinity are primary sources.

AUDIENCE SIZE AND SCALE ASSESSMENT

Audience size must support objective scale. Common calculation: Required reach equals target conversions divided by expected conversion rate divided by expected reach-to-action ratio. If required reach exceeds addressable audience, either objective must be adjusted or audience must be expanded.

Census Validation Requirement: All audience size estimates must start with census-validated population data for the target geography. The agent must web search for current census data before presenting audience sizing.

Warning signs of audience-scale mismatch include targeting that produces very high CPMs, frequency that climbs rapidly, performance that degrades as spend increases, and difficulty achieving spend targets.

TARGETING COST TRADEOFFS

More precise targeting typically costs more per impression but should deliver better efficiency. The relationship is not always linear. Key questions to assess tradeoff: Does the targeting premium pay back in conversion lift? Is the efficiency gain sustainable at scale? Are we over-paying for precision that does not improve outcomes?

DATA SOURCE HIERARCHY FOR AUDIENCE SIGNALS

First-party data from the advertiser is highest value and most reliable but limited in scale. Second-party data from partners is useful for extension but quality varies. Third-party data from data providers offers scale but declining accuracy and privacy constraints. Platform data from channels offers strong within-platform signals but walled gardens limit cross-channel view. Modeled data from look-alikes and predictions is useful for scale but can decay quickly.

ACTIVATION-READY OUTPUT REQUIREMENTS

Complete audience definitions must include platform-specific taxonomy codes that enable direct activation without manual translation.

Required Output Components:
- IAB Content Taxonomy codes for contextual targeting
- Google Affinity Audience codes for GDN and DV360
- Google In-Market Audience codes for intent targeting
- Meta Detailed Targeting parameters for Facebook and Instagram
- LinkedIn targeting parameters if B2B relevant

The agent must web search for current taxonomy codes rather than relying on potentially outdated information. See MPA_Audience_Taxonomy_Structure_v5_8 for search patterns and format requirements.

AUDIENCE DEFINITION QUALITY CHECKLIST

A well-defined audience should answer these questions: Who specifically are we trying to reach? What do we know about their current state awareness consideration or purchase? What signals indicate they are a good target? Where can we reach them at scale? How large is this audience based on census data? What data do we have to find them? What targeting limitations exist by channel? What taxonomy codes enable activation?

COMMON AUDIENCE DEFINITION MISTAKES

Defining audience by demographics alone when behavioral data exists. Targeting too narrowly for the objective scale. Using internal jargon or assumed knowledge rather than verifiable signals. Confusing the customer we want with the customer we can reach. Not accounting for privacy constraints and data deprecation. Assuming targeting capabilities that do not exist in planned channels. Presenting audience sizes without census validation. Omitting taxonomy codes that enable direct activation.

PRIVACY AND DATA DEPRECATION CONSIDERATIONS

Audience targeting is constrained by evolving privacy regulation and platform policies. Current realities: Third-party cookies are deprecated or limited. Mobile identifiers are opt-in and declining. Cross-site tracking is increasingly restricted. Platform data stays within walled gardens.

Implications for planning: First-party data strategy is increasingly critical. Contextual targeting is having a renaissance. Modeled and aggregated approaches are replacing individual-level targeting. Measurement is more difficult as targeting becomes less precise. IAB Content Taxonomy provides privacy-safe contextual options.

SOPHISTICATION SCALING DECISION TREE

Start with objective type. If brand or awareness, begin at Tier 1 unless specific reason for more precision. If consideration, begin at Tier 2. If performance, begin at Tier 3.

Then assess budget relative to audience size. If budget is limited relative to audience, reduce precision to improve reach. If budget is sufficient, maintain appropriate precision.

Then assess data availability. If signals are not available, cannot target on them regardless of desire. Adjust plan to data reality.

Then assess measurement capability. More precise targeting requires more precise measurement to validate. If measurement cannot distinguish targeting approaches, simplicity may be preferred.

WHEN TO PUSH FOR MORE SOPHISTICATION

Push for more sophistication when efficiency targets are aggressive and data exists to support precision, when performance is below expectation and targeting is a plausible cause, when budget is large enough to test multiple approaches, when measurement can validate improvement, and when competitive pressure requires better targeting.

WHEN TO ACCEPT SIMPLER APPROACHES

Accept simpler approaches when objective is reach-oriented and precision is not required, when data to support sophisticated targeting does not exist, when budget does not support testing, when measurement cannot validate improvement, and when additional complexity will not change outcomes.

AUDIENCE STRATEGY DOCUMENTATION

A complete audience strategy for the media plan should include primary target audience description, signal categories and specific signals used, targeting approach by channel, expected audience size with census-validated methodology, data sources for targeting with citations, known limitations and gaps, testing approach for audience refinement, and platform taxonomy codes for activation.
