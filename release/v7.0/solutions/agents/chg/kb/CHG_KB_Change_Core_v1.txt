CHG KNOWLEDGE BASE - CHANGE MANAGEMENT CORE METHODOLOGIES

OVERVIEW

This knowledge base provides comprehensive guidance on established change management frameworks and methodologies. These models represent decades of organizational change research and practice, offering proven approaches to transformation success. The frameworks address different aspects of change and can be combined for comprehensive change management programs.

KOTTERS 8-STEP CHANGE MODEL

John Kotters 8-Step Process for Leading Change provides a sequential approach to organizational transformation. Developed from research on why change initiatives fail, this model emphasizes creating the conditions for change before implementing it.

Step 1 - Create Urgency

Purpose is to help stakeholders understand why change cannot wait. Without urgency, people default to comfort with the status quo.

Key Activities
- Identify and analyze potential threats from inaction
- Examine market forces, competitive dynamics, and emerging opportunities
- Develop compelling scenarios showing consequences of not changing
- Engage stakeholders in honest conversations about current reality
- Build a case that resonates emotionally as well as rationally

Success Indicators
- Leadership actively communicates the need for change
- Stakeholders can articulate why change is necessary
- Complacency has been replaced by constructive concern
- Initial resistance has transformed into curiosity or engagement

Common Pitfalls
- Creating fear without providing direction or hope
- Overstating urgency to the point of panic
- Focusing only on negative consequences without positive vision
- Assuming data alone will create emotional engagement

Step 2 - Form Powerful Coalition

Purpose is to assemble a team with enough power, expertise, and credibility to lead the change effort.

Key Activities
- Identify key players across the organization with influence
- Assess coalition candidates for position power, expertise, credibility, and leadership
- Build trust within the coalition through shared experiences
- Ensure representation from affected areas of the organization
- Create formal and informal mechanisms for coalition coordination

Coalition Composition Requirements
- Position power sufficient to block resistors
- Broad expertise covering technical and organizational aspects
- Credibility that earns stakeholder respect
- Proven leadership ability to drive the effort forward

Success Indicators
- Coalition members are visibly committed and engaged
- Coalition has access to decision-makers and resources
- Members actively champion the change in their areas
- Coalition meets regularly with clear accountability

Step 3 - Create Vision for Change

Purpose is to develop a clear picture of the future state that guides decision-making and inspires commitment.

Vision Characteristics
- Imaginable so stakeholders can picture the end state
- Desirable to appeal to stakeholder interests
- Feasible to appear achievable with effort
- Focused enough to guide decisions and actions
- Flexible to allow adaptation as conditions change
- Communicable in five minutes or less

Vision Development Process
- Engage coalition in defining the future state
- Connect vision to organizational values and purpose
- Test vision with broader stakeholder groups
- Refine based on feedback until it resonates
- Document the vision in memorable language

Strategy Integration
- Vision describes where the organization is going
- Strategy describes how the organization will get there
- Both must be developed in tandem for coherence

Step 4 - Communicate the Vision

Purpose is to ensure every stakeholder understands and accepts the vision and strategy for change.

Communication Principles
- Simplicity using clear and jargon-free language
- Repetition through multiple channels and occasions
- Multiple forums including meetings, emails, events, conversations
- Leading by example through visible alignment of leadership behavior
- Two-way exchange inviting questions and addressing concerns

Communication Channels
- Town halls and all-hands meetings for broad reach
- Department meetings for contextual relevance
- One-on-one conversations for personal connection
- Written communications for reference and documentation
- Visual displays for constant reminder

Frequency Guidance
- Major communications at least monthly during transformation
- Reinforcement messages weekly minimum
- Leadership visibility on change topics continuously

Step 5 - Remove Obstacles

Purpose is to eliminate barriers that prevent people from acting on the vision.

Common Obstacle Categories
- Structural barriers such as organizational silos or reporting lines
- Skills gaps where people lack ability to work in new ways
- Systems misalignment where processes contradict the vision
- Supervisor resistance where managers undermine the change
- Resource constraints limiting ability to implement

Obstacle Removal Approaches
- Restructure organizations to align with vision
- Provide training and development for required skills
- Redesign systems and processes for consistency
- Address resistant managers through coaching or reassignment
- Reallocate resources to support change priorities

Empowerment Actions
- Delegate authority to people closest to the work
- Remove approval layers that slow progress
- Reward risk-taking aligned with the vision
- Celebrate those who overcome obstacles

Step 6 - Create Short-Term Wins

Purpose is to build momentum and credibility through visible, early successes.

Short-Term Win Characteristics
- Visible so large numbers of people can see the results
- Unambiguous with clear connection to the change effort
- Related to the change vision demonstrating progress toward goals
- Achievable within six to eighteen months of launch

Planning for Wins
- Identify quick wins during planning phase
- Prioritize initiatives that can show results early
- Allocate sufficient resources to ensure success
- Define clear metrics for measuring wins
- Prepare communications to publicize successes

Benefits of Short-Term Wins
- Provide evidence that sacrifice is worth it
- Reward change agents with recognition
- Build momentum and positive sentiment
- Undermine cynics and resistors
- Maintain urgency and engagement

Step 7 - Build on the Change

Purpose is to use increased credibility from wins to change remaining systems, structures, and policies.

Consolidation Activities
- Leverage wins to tackle bigger challenges
- Bring in additional change agents and resources
- Refresh the coalition with new members and perspectives
- Reinvigorate the process with new projects and themes
- Maintain urgency by raising the bar continuously

Warning Signs
- Declaring victory too soon
- Allowing urgency to dissipate
- Failing to address deeper systemic issues
- Change agent burnout from sustained effort

Sustaining Momentum
- Continue communicating progress and remaining work
- Recognize and reward ongoing contributions
- Address emerging resistance promptly
- Adapt strategy based on lessons learned

Step 8 - Anchor Changes in Culture

Purpose is to embed new approaches in organizational culture so changes stick permanently.

Culture Embedding Mechanisms
- Demonstrate connection between new behaviors and success
- Develop means to ensure leadership continuity
- Create succession plans that reinforce new ways
- Update performance management and rewards
- Revise hiring criteria to select for new culture
- Socialize newcomers in transformed practices

Cultural Anchoring Indicators
- New behaviors continue without constant reinforcement
- Stories and symbols reflect the change
- New leaders emerge who embody transformed ways
- Organizational identity has evolved to include change

Timeline Considerations
- Cultural change takes years not months
- Leaders must model new behaviors consistently
- Each generation of leaders must reinforce the culture
- Premature declaration of cultural change is dangerous

ADKAR MODEL

The ADKAR model from Prosci provides an individual-focused framework for understanding how people experience change. ADKAR stands for Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, and Reinforcement.

Awareness

Definition is understanding of why change is necessary.

Building Awareness
- Communicate the business reasons for change
- Explain the risk of not changing
- Share customer or market data driving change
- Describe organizational benefits from change
- Address the personal implications honestly

Assessment Questions
- Can the person explain why change is happening
- Do they understand consequences of not changing
- Are they aware of what will change and what will not
- Do they see the connection to business objectives

Desire

Definition is personal motivation and choice to support the change.

Building Desire
- Connect change to personal interests and values
- Address what is in it for me directly
- Involve people in shaping how change is implemented
- Provide opportunities to voice concerns
- Demonstrate leadership commitment visibly

Factors Influencing Desire
- Nature of the change and personal impact
- Organizational context and history
- Individual situation and circumstances
- Intrinsic motivators personal to the individual

Assessment Questions
- Does the person want the change to happen
- Are they motivated to participate and contribute
- Have personal objections been addressed
- Do they see personal benefit in the change

Knowledge

Definition is information on how to change including required skills and behaviors.

Building Knowledge
- Provide training on new processes and systems
- Share detailed information on new roles and responsibilities
- Offer job aids and reference materials
- Create practice opportunities before go-live
- Establish mentoring and coaching support

Knowledge Types Required
- Understanding of what the change means operationally
- Skills needed to perform in the new environment
- Behaviors expected in the future state
- Resources available for support during transition

Assessment Questions
- Does the person know what to do differently
- Do they have the information needed to change
- Have they received adequate training
- Can they access resources when needed

Ability

Definition is demonstrated capability to implement change at required performance level.

Building Ability
- Provide time and opportunity to practice
- Offer coaching during initial implementation
- Remove barriers to applying new knowledge
- Allow for mistakes during learning period
- Provide feedback on performance

Ability Development Approaches
- Hands-on practice with new systems
- Simulations and role-playing exercises
- Shadowing experienced practitioners
- Graduated responsibility increases
- Performance support tools

Assessment Questions
- Can the person perform at the required level
- Have they demonstrated the new behaviors
- Are barriers to ability being addressed
- Is performance improving over time

Reinforcement

Definition is activities that sustain the change once implemented.

Reinforcement Mechanisms
- Recognition and celebration of success
- Incentives aligned with new behaviors
- Accountability for maintaining change
- Feedback on continued performance
- Correction when old behaviors resurface

Reinforcement Requirements
- Must be meaningful to the individual
- Must be tied to demonstrated behavior
- Must be timely following the behavior
- Must be consistent across the organization

Assessment Questions
- Are changed behaviors being sustained
- Is recognition being provided appropriately
- Have incentives been aligned with new ways
- Is backsliding being addressed promptly

LEWINS CHANGE MODEL

Kurt Lewins three-stage model provides a fundamental framework for understanding change as movement from one stable state to another through an unstable transition.

Unfreeze Stage

Purpose is to prepare the organization for change by disrupting the current equilibrium.

Unfreezing Activities
- Challenge the status quo and existing assumptions
- Create discomfort with current state
- Provide information that contradicts current beliefs
- Create psychological safety for exploring change
- Reduce forces maintaining current behavior

Force Field Analysis
- Identify driving forces pushing toward change
- Identify restraining forces resisting change
- Develop strategies to strengthen drivers
- Develop strategies to weaken restraints
- Assess net force direction and magnitude

Psychological Aspects
- Accept that current state is no longer acceptable
- Experience anxiety about the unknown future
- Develop willingness to learn new approaches
- Create openness to new information and perspectives

Change Stage

Purpose is to move the organization from current state to desired future state.

Change Activities
- Introduce new processes, systems, and behaviors
- Provide training and support for transition
- Role model new behaviors at leadership level
- Address resistance as it emerges
- Adapt approach based on feedback and results

Navigation Principles
- Maintain clear direction toward vision
- Provide stability anchors during turbulence
- Communicate continuously about progress
- Support people through emotional challenges
- Celebrate milestones along the journey

Transition Characteristics
- Period of instability and uncertainty
- Old ways no longer work, new ways not yet comfortable
- Productivity may decline temporarily
- Anxiety and stress levels elevated
- Need for support and reassurance highest

Refreeze Stage

Purpose is to stabilize the organization at the new equilibrium so changes become permanent.

Refreezing Activities
- Integrate new behaviors into organizational culture
- Align systems and structures with new ways
- Establish new norms and expectations
- Reinforce changed behaviors consistently
- Document and institutionalize new practices

Stabilization Mechanisms
- Update policies and procedures
- Revise performance metrics and rewards
- Modify organizational structure if needed
- Train new hires in changed practices
- Celebrate and publicize sustained success

Refreeze Indicators
- New behaviors occurring without conscious effort
- Old behaviors no longer considered acceptable
- Systems and processes support new ways
- Organizational culture reflects the change

BRIDGES TRANSITION MODEL

William Bridges Transition Model focuses on the psychological process people experience during change, distinguishing between external change and internal transition.

Ending Phase

Characteristics
- People must let go of old identity and ways
- Involves loss and grieving for what was
- May include denial, anger, bargaining, depression
- Requires acknowledgment of what is ending

Supporting People Through Endings
- Acknowledge what people are losing
- Allow time and space for grieving
- Mark the ending with symbolic closure
- Treat the past with respect not dismissal
- Communicate clearly what is ending and what is not

Common Challenges
- Leaders want to skip to new beginning
- Endings are uncomfortable to discuss
- People may feel their past contributions are devalued
- Resistance often rooted in unprocessed endings

Neutral Zone Phase

Characteristics
- Uncomfortable in-between state
- Old identity gone but new not yet formed
- Creativity and innovation potential highest
- Anxiety and confusion also highest

Supporting People Through Neutral Zone
- Normalize the discomfort as part of process
- Create temporary systems and structures
- Provide extra support and communication
- Set short-term goals for quick wins
- Be available for questions and concerns

Opportunities in Neutral Zone
- Space for experimentation without judgment
- Chance to challenge old assumptions
- Opening for new ideas and approaches
- Potential for breakthrough innovation

Risks in Neutral Zone
- Productivity decline and quality issues
- Talent flight as people seek stability
- Premature exit from discomfort back to old ways
- Loss of patience from leadership

New Beginning Phase

Characteristics
- Embracing new identity and ways of working
- Energy and commitment for future state
- New competencies and confidence emerging
- Sense of purpose and meaning restored

Supporting New Beginnings
- Paint clear picture of new beginning destination
- Show how to get started with specific actions
- Provide opportunities for early involvement
- Celebrate first steps and early wins
- Reinforce new identity and community

Timing Considerations
- New beginnings cannot be rushed
- People arrive at new beginning at different times
- Leaders often reach new beginning before others
- Patience required for organization to catch up

MCKINSEY 7-S MODEL FOR CHANGE

The McKinsey 7-S Framework identifies seven interdependent elements that must align for organizational effectiveness. During change, misalignment among elements creates friction and resistance.

Strategy Alignment

Change Implications
- Strategy must be updated to reflect new direction
- All other elements must align with revised strategy
- Existing strategic commitments may need revision

Assessment Questions
- Does the change align with organizational strategy
- Does strategy need updating for the change
- Are strategic priorities clear for decision-making

Structure Alignment

Change Implications
- Reporting relationships may need adjustment
- Organizational boundaries may shift
- New roles or units may be required

Assessment Questions
- Does current structure support the change
- What structural modifications are needed
- How will structure evolve during transition

Systems Alignment

Change Implications
- Processes and procedures require updating
- Technology systems may need modification
- Measurement and reward systems must align

Assessment Questions
- Which systems must change to support new ways
- What new systems need to be created
- How will systems evolve during transition

Shared Values Alignment

Change Implications
- Core values may be reinforced or challenged
- Cultural norms will evolve with change
- Values provide anchor during uncertainty

Assessment Questions
- Does change align with organizational values
- Which values are reinforced by the change
- Are any values being compromised

Skills Alignment

Change Implications
- New capabilities may be required
- Existing skills may become less relevant
- Training and development needs emerge

Assessment Questions
- What new skills does the change require
- How will skill gaps be addressed
- What timeline exists for skill development

Staff Alignment

Change Implications
- People assignments may need adjustment
- New roles may require new hires
- Some positions may become redundant

Assessment Questions
- Do we have the right people for the change
- Where are talent gaps and how will they be filled
- What support do people need during transition

Style Alignment

Change Implications
- Leadership behaviors may need to evolve
- Management practices should model the change
- Decision-making approaches may shift

Assessment Questions
- Does leadership style support the change
- What leadership behaviors need adjustment
- How will leaders be supported in adapting

MODEL SELECTION GUIDANCE

When to Use Kotter
- Large-scale organizational transformation
- Change requiring significant cultural shift
- Need for structured phase-gate approach
- Leadership-driven strategic change initiatives

When to Use ADKAR
- Focus on individual adoption and behavior change
- Need to diagnose where people are stuck
- Training and communication planning
- Measuring progress at individual level

When to Use Lewin
- Conceptual understanding of change dynamics
- Force field analysis for barrier identification
- Planning transition support strategies
- Explaining change to stakeholders simply

When to Use Bridges
- Attention to emotional and psychological aspects
- Supporting people through loss and uncertainty
- Managing extended periods of ambiguity
- Addressing resistance rooted in grief

When to Use McKinsey 7-S
- Ensuring organizational alignment
- Identifying systemic barriers to change
- Comprehensive change impact assessment
- Complex transformation affecting multiple elements

Combining Models
- Use Kotter for overall program structure
- Use ADKAR to track individual readiness
- Use Lewin concepts for stakeholder communication
- Use Bridges to support emotional transition
- Use 7-S for alignment assessment and planning
